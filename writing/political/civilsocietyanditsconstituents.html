§ Civil Society and its Constituents

A political meditation on the preconditions for civil society.

Home About Writing GitHub

June 30, 2025

I. Introduction

Recent developments in American foreign policy—particularly the ongoing instability involving Iran and Israel—have served to underscore a broader political realignment underway in the United States. As the outlines of a post-2020s party system begin to emerge, it has become increasingly evident that both major political coalitions are entering a period of deep uncertainty. The Democratic Party, long characterized by internal ideological divisions, now finds itself in search of a viable contender to oppose the reemergence of a former president—an individual whose unique fusion of media spectacle, economic populism, and cultural grievance continues to command intense loyalty among a sizable portion of the electorate.

Within this environment, the boundaries of legitimate dissent have grown increasingly narrow. Criticism of the former president is frequently dismissed as obsession; skepticism of the opposition is met with accusations of betrayal. In such a climate, one might reasonably ask whether the underlying aims of our political struggle have been forgotten altogether.

Public discourse has, in many respects, drifted away from substantive engagement. It now resembles performance—less a contest between competing visions of the good, and more a symbolic clash of identities. And yet, beneath the noise, a quieter set of questions remains:

What is the purpose of civil society?

Who are its true constituents—not merely in name, but in shared responsibility?

And what obligations do we bear toward one another in a pluralistic and democratic state?

This paper does not seek to offer a partisan critique. Rather, it is a reflection on the structural and moral foundations of liberal society. In a time of deep division, it is worth reconsidering what it means to inhabit a shared space—one in which rights compete, obligations conflict, and the preservation of freedom requires not absolute autonomy, but a willingness to engage in trade-offs negotiated in good faith.

If liberal democracy is to endure, it will not be on the basis of rhetorical appeals to freedom or justice, abstracted from lived experience. It will depend on our ability to understand the preconditions that make such ideals sustainable—and to accept the burdens they place upon us when we live among those who do not share our views.

II. The Death of Good Faith

Among the many assumptions that undergird liberal democracy, few are as vital—and as fragile—as the presumption of good faith. Though the Founding Fathers could not eliminate factionalism, they sought to temper its excesses by appealing to shared civic virtue. James Madison, for instance, warned of the dangers posed by majority passions and advocated institutional checks as a means of preserving republican order. Today, however, the political landscape increasingly reflects a breakdown of this foundational trust. Public discourse is no longer structured around disagreement between competing visions of the good, but around mutual suspicion, in which each side regards the other not as mistaken, but as fundamentally dangerous.

It is tempting to believe that institutions alone are sufficient to safeguard liberty. Yet no legal system, no constitutional mechanism, can restrain a populace committed to acting in bad faith. Where trust dissolves, rules become performative; law is reduced to compliance under threat rather than legitimacy under consensus. In such a condition, the state is left with little more than the capacity to coerce. The question, then, is what remains as the motivating force of civic life—what serves as the “carrot” when shared belief has decayed? At present, it is not clear that one exists.

Extremes on both ends of the political spectrum increasingly adopt similar modes of engagement. Among segments of the online left, even minor dissent may result in reputational censure; ideological purity becomes a prerequisite for inclusion. In parallel, the populist right, particularly in its more fervent manifestations, demands loyalty over principle, and regards deviation with hostility. These patterns do not represent a healthy pluralism; they signify a collapse of presumed decency—the civic courtesy of believing that others, even when mistaken, may still be acting in pursuit of the common good.

This is not to suggest that public frustration is without cause. The conditions that enabled the political rise of Donald Trump were not illusory; they emerged from authentic disaffection, cultural alienation, and a sense of systemic neglect. But there is a difference between acknowledging pain and weaponizing it. Collective grievance, however emotionally compelling, cannot substitute for civic repair. At some point, both citizens and institutions must reckon with a difficult question: Are we operating in good faith—or are we driven primarily by resentment, fear, or antagonism toward those we do not fully understand?

Though I do not profess Christian belief, I find certain scriptural insights philosophically instructive. “The wrath of man worketh not the righteousness of God” (James 1:20) speaks to the danger of moral certainty unaccompanied by humility. Outrage, however justified it may feel, has the power to obscure nuance and erode judgment. When we lose the ability to distinguish between disagreement and malice, we forfeit the possibility of deliberation and revert to tribalism.

Most individuals, I believe, act from a sincere conviction that their actions serve the public good. The failure lies not in motive, but in perception: few are willing to extend the same sincerity to those who oppose them. This, more than ideological division itself, marks the true death of good faith.

III. Freedom as a Trade-Off

Thomas Sowell is often quoted as saying, “There are no solutions. There are only trade-offs.” While this observation is frequently cited in political discourse, it is rarely treated with the seriousness it warrants. Too often, the phrase is invoked to deflect criticism or terminate discussion, rather than to invite a deeper examination of what liberty requires in practice. Yet if taken seriously, it exposes a central tension in modern governance: that every freedom we enjoy is conditioned by structures that also constrain us.

To illustrate this point, imagine an individual living alone on a remote island. There is no state, no legal apparatus, no neighbors, no taxes—no external authority of any kind. One is, in this hypothetical scenario, utterly and indisputably free. But what does that freedom entail?

In such conditions, one may act without interference. One may attempt to build a raft, gather food, create shelter, or cry out into the wilderness. One is free to try—and free to fail. What one lacks, however, is any form of protection or support. There is no freedom from illness, from injury, from exposure, from isolation, or from despair. What exists is negative liberty—the absence of coercion—but not positive liberty, which entails the presence of capacity, opportunity, and security.

This distinction is essential:

  • Freedom to refers to the absence of external constraint.
  • Freedom from refers to the presence of enabling conditions—social, material, and institutional—that make meaningful choice possible.

Modern political life depends upon both.

Consider some common examples of “freedom from” in contemporary society:

  • Freedom from infectious disease.
  • Freedom from arbitrary violence.
  • Freedom from autocratic rule.
  • Freedom from unsafe infrastructure or unregulated food and medicine.
  • Freedom from systemic illiteracy or lack of educational access.

These freedoms are not the products of spontaneous order. They are the results of deliberate social investment—secured by law, maintained by institutions, and funded through collective sacrifice. They require coordination, oversight, and, inevitably, regulation.

Each of these protections is also generative. For every “freedom from,” there is a corresponding “freedom to”:

  • Freedom from illiteracy enables the freedom to read, to express, and to participate in public life.
  • Freedom from foodborne illness enables the freedom to consume safely and with confidence.
  • Freedom from lawlessness enables the freedom to speak, worship, and assemble.

Such benefits are not without cost. But the notion of a purely minimalist state—one in which government involvement is reduced to the vanishing point—fails to account for the interdependence required to sustain even basic freedoms. The conditions of modern life, from the buildings we inhabit to the roads we travel and the technologies we rely upon, are all subject to regulatory frameworks. These frameworks, though imperfect, are not imposed arbitrarily. They exist because risk is real, and because the consequences of neglect can be profound.

To view regulation merely as restriction is to misunderstand its purpose. At its best, regulation is a form of risk management—a mechanism through which society curbs reckless behavior, prevents foreseeable harm, and fosters trust in public systems. This is not to deny that regulation can overreach. Bureaucracy can become inefficient, policies can be poorly designed, and oversight can be captured by special interests. These concerns merit serious scrutiny. But to reject regulation categorically is not an argument grounded in principle; it is an abdication of realism.

The pursuit of liberty, therefore, must not be imagined as escape from society, but as a form of life enabled by it. The ideal is not the isolated autonomy of the castaway, but the shared capacity to act meaningfully within a stable and cooperative order.

Put simply, liberty is not diminished by structure. It is made possible by it.

IV. Freedom and Religion

The relationship between freedom and religion has long been marked by complexity. While both claim to provide guiding frameworks for human conduct, they frequently approach the question of moral authority from fundamentally different perspectives. Religion, particularly in its revealed forms, often grounds moral obligation in divine command. Liberal societies, by contrast, tend to frame moral responsibility in terms of personal autonomy and mutual respect within pluralistic communities.

My own position on this matter is shaped by a moral philosophy known as emotivism. According to this view, when individuals make moral claims—such as, for instance, “homosexuality is a sin”—they are not stating objective truths, but rather expressing evaluative attitudes. In its most distilled form, such a statement becomes: “homosexuality—nay.” Moral discourse, on this account, is not akin to mathematics or empirical science. It is a reflection of deeply embedded intuitions and social norms—felt, learned, and historically contingent.

From this standpoint, I find the claim that Christian moral frameworks are objectively grounded to be philosophically unpersuasive. If the God of Christian theism exists and is capable of inscribing moral law onto the human heart—as passages such as Romans 2:15 suggest—then one is left to wonder why divine will remains so persistently subject to interpretive conflict. If God intends for moral truth to be known and followed, why is that truth not made unmistakably clear? Why, instead, has so much of human history been marked by theological division and moral uncertainty?

Thus, when believers claim they are “following Jesus’ teachings” or “doing what God wants,” I do not interpret such assertions as statements of certainty, but as confessions of faith—necessarily filtered through human limitations, cultural assumptions, and fallible reasoning. My only request, as a fellow participant in moral inquiry, is for clarity and candor.

I will offer the same in return.

My own moral framework is not grounded in revelation. It emerges from personal experience, sustained reflection, and intuitive response. I do not claim to possess ultimate knowledge of what is right. I only know what feels coherent and just, given the circumstances I have lived. I do not claim moral authority—only sincerity.

It is important to note that my critique of certain forms of Christian moral reasoning is not intended to disparage the individuals who hold such views. In fact, I believe many Christians—especially those committed to fundamentalist interpretations—are motivated by a sincere desire to reduce harm, preserve order, and remain faithful to what they perceive as divine instruction. They are, in their own way, striving for the good.

Nor is such striving exclusive to the religious. The progressive activist, the secular philosopher, the libertarian dissenter—all are, in different ways, pursuing a vision of justice. What is striking—and concerning—is how rarely we extend to one another the presumption of moral earnestness. Increasingly, we view those who disagree with us not as principled individuals arriving at different conclusions, but as enemies, obstructing a path we believe is obvious.

This loss of trust is not merely a cultural tragedy; it is a threat to civic stability. The task before us is not to agree on moral conclusions, but to recover the possibility of mutual understanding. As an example: I would ask the Christian reader to consider whether the transgender individual might also be seeking a better world—one rooted in dignity, peace, and acceptance. And I would ask the secular progressive to consider whether the devout Christian might not be clinging blindly to tradition, but rather attempting, with humility, to live in accordance with what they believe to be a higher order. In both cases, the goal is not agreement, but recognition of the other as a fellow moral agent.

This, I would argue, is where civil society must begin again: not in consensus, but in the renewal of imaginative empathy—the capacity to enter, however briefly, into the worldview of another.

What does this have to do with freedom?

Interestingly, religion—particularly in its legalistic or covenantal forms—offers a conception of liberty that may still hold value. In many traditions, the principle is clear: individuals are free to act unless a specific action is expressly forbidden. This mirrors the negative-liberty tradition found in constitutional law, in which freedom is not defined exhaustively, but rather structured by exceptions. The American legal system, since Marbury v. Madison, has largely operated according to this logic—reacting to specific challenges, rather than issuing comprehensive ethical directives in advance.

In this sense, religious and constitutional traditions converge: both acknowledge that liberty requires boundaries. Moral law, like legal precedent, is not a map so much as a process—reactive, interpretive, and always subject to revision in light of new understanding.

Although I do not personally affirm divine law, I can appreciate the structure it provides. Freedom without form becomes chaos. Liberty without mutual obligation becomes license. The moral contours of religious life may, at their best, remind us that the right to act must be tempered by a responsibility to others.

V. The Paradox of Social Benefit

One of the most underappreciated truths of civic life is that, in many cases, by helping others, we help ourselves. This is not merely a moral aspiration, nor a vague appeal to karmic balance. It is a material, structural fact. When public infrastructure is funded, when educational institutions are strengthened, when others are housed, vaccinated, or nourished, the society we all inhabit becomes more stable, more prosperous, and more livable.

This presents a paradox for the individualist framework: that in ceding a measure of autonomy, we often acquire greater freedom in return—freedom from uncertainty, from danger, from disrepair. Our limitations, in a well-functioning civil society, are not solely defined by individual ability. They are bounded—or expanded—by the collective capacities of the institutions and relationships to which we belong.

I do not write this as a Marxist, nor as a socialist. I do not believe in the inevitable collapse of capitalism, nor do I accept the premises of dialectical materialism. These labels, when applied casually, often reflect more suspicion than understanding. My view is simpler, and perhaps more enduring: that the strength of a free society lies in the mutual uplift of its members; that the well-being of the individual is often inseparable from the well-being of the group; and that we are able to build far more together than we ever could alone.

Consider a road. Its existence benefits the business owner, the commuter, the teacher, the emergency responder, the parent, and the student. Consider public health: when others are treated, our collective risk is reduced. Consider education: when literacy and skill are widespread, economies flourish. Even those who advocate most strongly for independence and self-reliance rely upon publicly maintained systems—law enforcement, intellectual property protections, regulated transportation, and digital infrastructure.

These systems are not luxuries. They are not handouts. They are investments—investments in the civic fabric that sustains modern life. And like any sound investment, they yield returns. They benefit not only the vulnerable, but also the secure, not only the dependent, but also those who imagine themselves self-sufficient.

One does not need to be a collectivist to recognize this. One only needs to be honest.

The paradox of social benefit is this: that our highest autonomy is made possible through our interdependence. The more we ensure that others are protected, educated, and supported, the more we expand the conditions under which liberty can be meaningfully exercised. This is not idealism. It is realism.

A society in which only the strong flourish is not truly free. It is simply a jungle dressed in constitutional language.

To make this point more concrete, consider the military. It is often held up as the embodiment of discipline, sacrifice, and national service. Yet the military is perhaps the clearest institutional example of collective benefit. No soldier thrives in isolation. Each depends upon coordination, logistics, and mutual trust. The unit matters more than the individual. The success of one depends on the strength of all.

I have spoken to veterans who understand this paradox intuitively. They have lived it. They know that their safety in combat often depended on the competence and care of others, just as the lives of others depended on them. In the field, individualism is not a virtue. It is a liability.

And yet, when these same veterans return home, they often find that the society they defended is unwilling to provide the same degree of care and coordination. Basic access to healthcare, housing, and reintegration support is frequently limited. We honor the idea of sacrifice in rhetoric while denying the structures that make such sacrifice sustainable in practice. This is not a failure of means. It is a failure of civic imagination.

The military reminds us that we do not survive alone. Civil society—at its best—is how we carry that insight forward into peacetime.

VI. Where Do We Go From Here?

We cannot act as if the other is evil.

We cannot act as if our individual vote does not matter.

We cannot act as if we are not a meaningful splash in the puddle of the population.

Because the moment we do, we lose the plot.

We lose the shared trust that makes disagreement tolerable. We lose the sense of agency that makes civic participation meaningful. And we lose the quiet dignity that comes from knowing that, however small our influence may seem, it does ripple outward.

That is what makes democracy—not perfect, but better. Better than all the rest.

Not because it guarantees virtue, but because it permits correction. Because it allows for messiness without collapsing into violence. Because it lets us fight with words instead of blood. Because it assumes that power must be distributed, not hoarded.

So—where do we go from here?

We begin with good faith—not blind trust, but the willingness to assume that others, even our opponents, might be acting on principle rather than malice.

We begin with trade-offs—recognizing that freedom is not absolute, and that in giving up a little, we often gain much more.

We begin with humility—acknowledging that none of us hold the full truth, and that every worldview is shaped by interpretation, history, and instinct.

We begin with honesty—about our motivations, our biases, our fears, and our hopes.

And we begin with the recognition that we are not alone. That we belong to a shared project—one that is old, fragile, and worth saving.

Civil society is not a slogan. It is a daily choice. It is built in trust, guarded by discourse, and made real through the quiet, deliberate act of assuming we are still in this together.

If we want to preserve freedom, then let us start acting like we deserve it.
