§ Ethocratism

A coined term on the architecture of group identity as moral power.

Home About Writing GitHub

Definition

A mode of governance, discourse, or moral reasoning wherein loyalty to a tribal identity—whether racial, ideological, cultural, sexual, &c.—supersedes universal principles, institutional neutrality, or individual merit.

Ethocratism prizes group allegiance above truth, and enforces its order through social coercion, moral absolutism, and the ritual purification of dissent.

Preface: On the Coinage of Ethocratism

This term is being developed by a student of Computer Science and Japanese, not as an academic authority, but as an observer of language, ideology, and the architecture of legitimacy in a world increasingly governed by identity over principle.

I’m using ChatGPT as a tool of refinement—not to produce ideas on my behalf, but to sharpen the ones I’ve already begun to form. Every phrase here is rooted in my own thought, clarified in dialogue, and offered in the spirit of intellectual transparency.

Over time, I’ve grown dissatisfied with the existing vocabulary available to describe the kinds of ideological behavior I see emerging across the cultural spectrum. Each term seems either too blunt, too historically misused, or too narrow in scope.

Terms I Reject

  • Fascism – too historically bound and rhetorically poisoned; now functions more as a slur than a diagnosis
  • Marxism – overused as a catch-all, often invoked without understanding dialectics or class theory
  • Wokeness – a floating signifier more useful for culture war ammunition than analytical precision
  • Tribalism – close in spirit, but too imprecise and primitive in connotation
  • Cult – too religious; suggests blind submission to a leader, not memetic decentralization
  • Groupthink – overly Orwellian; implies incapacity rather than strategic identity enforcement
  • Moralism / Moral Panic – accurate in tone, but insufficient for describing institutional power transfer
  • Identitarianism – tied too exclusively to far-right ideology, missing broader progressive forms

Why the Term Matters

“Ethocratism” names a system where group identity becomes the axis of moral and institutional legitimacy, and where power is asserted not through universal principle, but through identity enforcement.

Whether in DEI bureaucracy, online tribunals, or nationalist revivals, this mode of governance demands a new conceptual tool. This is that attempt.

Derived from the Greek ethnos (tribe, people) and kratos (power, rule), ethocratism means, simply, rule by the tribe.

This term is still evolving. I offer it not as a final word, but a starting point. In naming it, we make it visible— and once visible, we can critique, resist, or refine it.
